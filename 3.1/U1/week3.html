Compound Sentences

▾
A compound sentence = two simple sentences joined by a comma + a coordinating conjunction.
and — adds an idea but — contrasts or — gives a choice
Examples
I helped Mom cook, and she let me stir the batter.
Marco wants the red jacket, but his parents like the black one.
You can buy soup, or you can make a sandwich.
Run-on sentence — two sentences joined without a conjunction or comma.
Fragment — a sentence that is incomplete (missing subject or verb).

Spelling: Base Words & Endings -ed, -ing, -er, -est

▾
Verbs — add -ed / -ing
Doubling Rule (CVC) — double the last consonant, then add -ed or -ing.
e.g. hop → hopped → hopping
Dropping Rule (CVCe) — keep -ed for past tense; drop the e and add -ing for present.
e.g. hope → hoped → hoping
Base word+ ed+ ing
CVC (double last consonant)
hophoppedhopping
askaskedasking
run*ranrunning
CVCe (drop the e)
hopehopedhoping
smilesmiledsmiling
make*mademaking
consonant + y (change y → i)
carrycarriedcarrying
studystudiedstudying
trytriedtrying
Adjectives — add -er / -est
Doubling Rule (CVC) — double last consonant + -er / -est.
e.g. big → bigger → biggest
Dropping Rule (CVCe) — keep -er / -est (drop the e).
e.g. nice → nicer → nicest
Irregular: good → better → best  |  bad → worse → worst
